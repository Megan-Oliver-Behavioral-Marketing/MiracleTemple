Are You Speaking Life or Death With Your Words?

Preached by Pastor Elder Terence Oliver · Adapted by Megan Oliver · July 21, 2026 · Proverbs 18:21, Matthew 5:21-24, James 3:1-12

This week, Pastor Elder Terence Oliver preached a convicting message on the power of the tongue, teaching that our words are seeds — and every day, we choose whether we're sowing life or sowing death into the people around us, right here in our Byhalia, Mississippi community.

Key Takeaways

  • Words are not neutral — Scripture treats careless or cruel speech as a serious spiritual issue, not just a bad habit.
  • We are called to speak good seeds into the church, our own lives, our children, and our spouses.
  • Holding onto bitterness and malice blocks salvation, healing, and deliverance from moving in our lives — we have to let it go.
  • Even when circumstances look like defeat, don't let your words agree with the fight. Keep speaking life and hold on.

Why Does Scripture Take Our Words So Seriously?

Pastor Oliver opened with a sobering truth: name-calling and insults aren't just bad manners in God's eyes — they're a serious spiritual issue, often rooted in anger, malice, hurt, or a lack of self-control. Scripture doesn't treat this lightly. Jesus Himself compared insulting a brother, or calling someone a fool, to the sin of murder.

"But I say unto you, That whosoever is angry with his brother without a cause shall be in danger of the judgment: and whosoever shall say to his brother, Raca, shall be in danger of the council: but whosoever shall say, Thou fool, shall be in danger of hell fire." — Matthew 5:22

James 3 goes even further, describing the tongue as "a restless evil, full of deadly poison" — small, but powerful enough to set a person's whole life on fire, the way one spark can burn down a forest.

What Does It Mean to Speak "Good Seeds"?

Pastor Oliver's charge to the church was simple but weighty: we must speak good seeds into Miracle Temple, into our own lives, into our children's lives, and into our spouses' lives. Every word is a seed we're planting — and we will eat the fruit of what we've sown.

"Death and life are in the power of the tongue: and they that love it shall eat the fruit thereof." — Proverbs 18:21

He shared a story about the late Pastor Walter Shannon, who once corrected a church member for calling her husband a liar — even though, by her account, he was one. His point wasn't that she was wrong about the truth; it was that speaking it aloud, over and over, was a bad seed being sown into her marriage. Out of obedience, she stopped saying it.

Titus 3 echoes this same call to gentleness: believers are commanded to "speak evil of no one," to avoid quarreling, and to show meekness to all people — not because people always deserve it, but because that's who we've been called to be since God's kindness and love appeared to us.

How Do We Let Go of the Bitterness That Poisons Our Words?

The sermon turned to Ephesians 4, where Pastor Oliver preached passionately on releasing bitterness, wrath, anger, and malice — all the things that give our words their poison in the first place.

"Let all bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, be put away from you, with all malice... and be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you." — Ephesians 4:31-32

"Let it go," he urged the congregation — because holding onto malice blocks what God wants to do. Salvation, healing, and deliverance move in the house when strongholds are broken, and strongholds are broken when we release the bitterness we've been carrying.

Don't Let Your Words Agree With Defeat

Closing the message, Pastor Oliver reminded the church that every careless word will be accounted for on the day of judgment (Matthew 12:36) — but he also pointed to the wisdom of Proverbs 12:16: a fool's anger is known at once, but a wise person stays calm when insulted.

He pointed to the Canaanite woman in Matthew 15, who refused to let a discouraging word stop her from pressing in for her daughter's healing — and to a story about a boxer, bloodied and losing badly, whose coach had given up and thrown in the towel. But the fighter caught the towel and threw it right back. Something on the inside wouldn't agree with what looked like defeat on the outside — and he went on to win.

"That's the Holy Ghost working on the inside, showing up on the outside," Pastor Oliver said. "Don't let nobody talk negative about you. Tell them: I'm somebody. For God I live."

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the Bible treat insults and name-calling so seriously?

Scripture connects careless or cruel speech to deeper issues of the heart — anger, malice, and a lack of self-control. Jesus even compared calling someone a "fool" to the seriousness of murder, showing how much weight our words carry.

What does it mean to "speak good seeds"?

It means being intentional about using our words to build up rather than tear down — speaking life over our church, our own lives, our children, and our marriages, trusting that we'll eventually eat the fruit of what we've spoken.

How do I stop holding onto bitterness?

Pastor Oliver taught that letting go of bitterness, wrath, and malice (Ephesians 4:31-32) is a choice — one that clears the way for God to move in salvation, healing, and deliverance.

What can I do when circumstances look like defeat?

Like the boxer who refused to let his coach throw in the towel, we're called to keep speaking life and holding on in faith, even when things look bloody and difficult — trusting that the Holy Ghost working within will show up in our circumstances.
